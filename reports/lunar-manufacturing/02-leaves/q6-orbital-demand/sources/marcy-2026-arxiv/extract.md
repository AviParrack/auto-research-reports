## Abstract

Marcy projects that orbital computing infrastructure will be deployed
in sun-synchronous low-Earth orbit to address terrestrial AI power
constraints. The paper situates U.S. data center demand on a trajectory
beyond 100 GW by 2035 and global demand toward 1 TW. A reference 5 GW
orbital facility would require a solar array measuring 4×4 km at
approximately 500 km altitude. Marcy emphasizes the visibility-and-collision
consequences: the resulting structures would appear at magnitude
g = -5 to -7 (roughly 100× brighter than the brightest stars), span
approximately 0.4° on the sky, and be visible for roughly 1.5 hours
after sunset and 1.5 hours before sunrise. The paper raises astronomical,
technical, and cultural concerns about dozens of such structures
forming visible chains.

## Key claims

- us-data-center-power-2035: U.S. data center power demand projected
  "beyond 100 gigawatt by 2035" with global trajectory toward 1 TW.
- reference-5gw-facility: A 5 GW orbital facility requires solar arrays
  measuring 4 km × 4 km at approximately 500 km altitude.
- orbital-brightness: Magnitude g = -5 to -7 at the orbital tier;
  comparable to the Moon's apparent size (~0.4°).
- visibility-window: Visible 1.5 hours after sunset and 1.5 hours before
  sunrise from any given Earth location.
- collision-and-debris: Multiple such structures pose serious
  astronomical, technical, and cultural concerns including increased
  collision risk.
