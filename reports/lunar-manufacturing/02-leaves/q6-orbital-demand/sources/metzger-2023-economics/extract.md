## Abstract

Metzger develops a closed-form economic framework for in-space industry
where capital is launched from Earth, deployed and teleoperated on a
second body, and product is delivered back into space markets.
Applied to lunar-derived rocket propellant, the model identifies the
"gear ratio on cost" for transporting capital and the "production mass
ratio" (capital mass / lifetime product mass) as the two dominant
levers. The paper concludes lunar propellant production is plausibly
commercially viable under reasonable parameter assumptions, with
near-term demand of 450 tonnes/year of propellant (≈2,450 t/yr of
processed lunar water) generating ~$2.4B/yr in revenue. The mechanism
operates as a demand-elastic feedback: cheaper in-space propellant
lowers the cost of every downstream activity, expanding the propellant
market beyond the level launching all-Earth propellant would support.

## Key claims

- near-term-annual-demand-450t: "near-term annual demand of 450 metric
  tons of lunar derived propellant equating to 2,450 metric tons of
  processed lunar water" generating ~$2.4B/yr in revenue.
- gear-ratio-framework: Two parameters dominate viability — gear ratio
  Γ for capital transport and production mass ratio φ for capital mass
  per lifetime product mass. Threshold ~35× at GTO under mid-parameters.
- demand-elastic-feedback: Cheaper in-space propellant lowers cost of
  all downstream space activities, expanding the propellant market
  itself.
- gto-dominant-market: Geosynchronous transfer orbit servicing is the
  most economically attractive near-term propellant market because it
  saves spacecraft mass already targeted at high-value orbits.
- competitiveness-conditional: Commercial competitiveness depends on
  achieving sub-threshold gear ratio and production mass ratio — not on
  the propellant chemistry itself.
