## Abstract

NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman announced (March 24-25, 2026) a $20 billion plan over seven years to build a moon base near the lunar south pole. The base will feature habitats, pressurized rovers, and nuclear power systems. NASA simultaneously cancelled the Gateway lunar-orbit station and will redirect Gateway components to surface operations. Mission cadence target: two crewed lunar landings per year, with no fewer than two competing launch providers. Artemis 4 and 5 are targeted for 2028 with a 2028 Mars nuclear-reactor demonstration (Skyfall). Isaacman frames this as fitting within NASA's existing top-line budget by "repurposing hardware to focus on the moon and by trimming bureaucratic waste and inefficiency."

## Key statements

- 20b-7-years: "$20 billion over the next seven years to build a moon base near the lunar south pole"
- two-landings-per-year: "I envision launching two moon landing missions per year to establish semi-permanent astronaut occupation"
- not-flags-and-footprints: "This time, the goal is not flags and footprints. This time, the goal is to stay"
- crewed-landings-every-6-months: "We intend to work with no fewer than two launch providers with the aim of crewed landings every six months"
- existing-budget-claim: "NASA would be able to afford the new Artemis architecture, space nuclear power development, ongoing science missions and new exploration ventures, as well as working to facilitate the commercialization of low-Earth orbit, with its existing budget, repurposing hardware to focus on the moon and by trimming bureaucratic waste and inefficiency"
- gateway-cancelled: "NASA is canceling plans to deploy a space station in lunar orbit and will instead use its components to construct a $20 billion base on the moon's surface over the next seven years"
- phase-1-2-3: "Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3. If the next administration wants to dial back a little bit of Moon-based spending, no problem"

## Reviewer notes

Tier-B with maximal current policy weight — the sitting NASA Administrator's stated number is the de-facto current US-program-of-record for surface lunar infrastructure. $20B / 7 years implies ~$2.9B/yr sustained, which is consistent (within an order of magnitude) with both Metzger's "1/3 of national-space-program budgets" framing and Sowers' $4B commercial-architecture anchor. Critically: this is the on-top-of-Artemis-transportation-spend figure — it represents the surface-base capex layer specifically, not total lunar program cost. Combined with the existing $93B Artemis program-of-record through FY2025, the total US public-program lunar capex through ~2033 is approximately $93B + $20B + ongoing operations = $115-140B order-of-magnitude.
