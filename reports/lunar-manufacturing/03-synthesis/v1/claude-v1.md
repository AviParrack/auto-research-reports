# Lunar manufacturing crossover: a two-legged conditional answer

## Why this question matters

The interesting object is not whether humans can build factories on the Moon, but whether the resulting product flow becomes the cheaper path to orbital infrastructure. That decision changes everything downstream: where to deploy a trillion dollars of AI compute over the next two decades; whether to commit national-program funding to large-scale lunar ISRU versus terrestrial launch capacity expansion; how to read the strategic moves of SpaceX, Blue Origin, and the Chinese space program. The naive form of the question — "will lunar manufacturing be cheaper by 2040?" — admits an answer that the calc does not deliver. The honest form replaces the calendar year with an acceleration regime, then surfaces what binds the answer to a regime rather than a date.

## Where this synthesis fits

Seven leaves feed in: q1 (Earth launch cost), q2 (lunar ascent cost), q3 (ISRU feasibility), q4 (gear-ratio competitiveness framework), q5 (capital buildup), q6 (orbital demand), q7 (mass-driver feasibility). A cross-consistency pass over all seven [01-tree/cross-consistency-p01.md] surfaced no analytical contradictions — only scope-mismatches and regime-conditional dependencies, all reciprocally recorded. The framework that holds across the seven leaves is the **BAU / industrial-explosion / TAI / stall** decomposition; the answer to the root question varies by regime, not by calendar year, and the variation spans approximately three orders of magnitude.

## Headline answer

Lunar manufacturing beats Earth launch for orbital infrastructure in the **TAI-grade compression regime** — and only there. The crossover stands on **two independent legs**:

**(1) Cost.** Mass-driver delivery from the lunar surface to LEO under q2's late-era scenario [q2.c5] lands at **$50/kg** assuming $10B mass-driver capital. q7's independent first-principles derivation [q7.c6, q7.c8] says that $10B capital is achievable only under TAI-grade engineering compression — under business-as-usual the mass-driver capital is $1,242B and operational scale lands 20-30 years out. The cost crossover is therefore not "available by 2040" but **"available conditional on TAI-grade compression of the engineering envelope."** At that margin, lunar-mass-driver $50/kg sits within a factor of 2 of q1's optimistic-late Starship at $59/kg — a real crossover, not a notional one.

**(2) Throughput necessity.** Under TAI-compression demand assumptions [q6.c2, q6.c6], space-based data centre mass demand alone reaches ~2.67 Mt/yr by 2040, exceeding plausible Earth-launch throughput (~400 kt/yr at 200 active reusable Starships × 10 flights/yr × 200 t) by approximately an order of magnitude. Lunar-sourced bulk mass becomes architecturally necessary for roughly half the SDC mass budget — the structural half (radiators, solar arrays, station-keeping, sintered regolith shielding). The compute hardware half (GPUs, memory, networking, power conditioning) stays Earth-launch-bound regardless. The throughput leg is independent of the cost leg; even if mass-driver $/kg never converged to terrestrial parity, the supply-chain ceiling would still force the architecture.

Under **business-as-usual**, neither leg holds. Mass driver does not reach Mt-scale in any time horizon shorter than ~25 years [q7.c11]; demand stays well inside Earth-launch capacity. Under **stall**, demand fails entirely [q6.c7] — the thesis collapses for lack of buyers, not lack of supply.

**Confidence: medium overall.** The framework is high-confidence (BAU/IE/TAI/stall regime decomposition is consistent across all seven leaves; gear-ratio physics in q4 is high confidence). The headline numbers — $50/kg, $59/kg, $1,242B BAU, $13.3B TAI — are individually medium to medium-low confidence, anchored on sourced priors with substantial spread (q7's regime-compression factors and q5's $100k/kg aerospace-flight-hardware multiplier are speculative scenario priors). Two engineering risks bind the answer more tightly than the capital question does: the cycle-life gap (below) and the polar-ice gate (below).

## The two legs in detail

### Cost — assembled across q1, q2, q4, q7

q1's three Starship operational scenarios bracket Earth launch internal cost across the planning horizon: optimistic $59-277/kg, partial $107-466/kg (the B1076 34-reuse milestone in March 2026 [q1.c6] converted this from aspirational to operationally demonstrated for Falcon 9 hardware), pessimistic $194-878/kg. Under TAI/IE compression of refurbishment labor, cadence, and ground-operations cost [q1.c7], the optimistic case drops further to $15-25/kg internal. Falcon 9 customer list prices are independently rising at approximately $500/kg/yr (February 2026 baseline $7,000/kg rideshare, $3,240/kg dedicated launch on full payload utilisation [q1.c5]). The 10-15× gap between internal cost and list price is itself a load-bearing variable; we return to it under "regime split" below.

q4's gear-ratio framework [q4.c1, q4.c3, q4.c9] makes the structural lunar-vs-LEO accounting explicit. Under pure chemical reusable round-trip from the lunar surface, Γ_LEO ≈ 14: every kilogram of lunar product delivered to LEO requires fourteen kilograms of propellant in motion. This makes LEO the hardest cislunar destination for lunar product. Closer destinations are easier (LLO Γ ≈ 0.9, EML1 Γ ≈ 1.3, GEO Γ ≈ 1.4), but LEO is where the demand is. The architectural variable that closes the gap is **solar electric propulsion (SEP) with molecular water at Isp ≈ 2000s on the lunar-product return leg** [q4.c12], which drops Γ_LEO from 14 to approximately 1. Aerobraking on the Earth-approach leg is a partial alternative — q2 quantifies it as approximately 2-3× cost reduction across all chemical scenarios [q2.c4], "the dominant architectural lever for chemical-only delivery." Either way, the chemical-only-round-trip architecture is not viable for LEO; some combination of SEP, aerobraking, or both is required.

q2's three operational scenarios bracket lunar-surface-to-LEO cost across the same planning horizon: chemical Earth-imports-only $1,303-13,029/kg, chemical aggressive-ISRU $994-8,912/kg, mass-driver+SEP $50-528/kg [q2.c3, q2.c5]. The chemical scenarios are an order of magnitude or more above q1's terrestrial denominator under every era and every regime. **Only mass-driver+SEP at late-era throughput closes the multiple to within a factor of 2** of q1's optimistic-late Starship cost. Late-era chemical aggressive-ISRU at $994/kg sits roughly 9× above q1's partial-late $107/kg and 17× above q1's optimistic-late $59/kg. This is the structural framing: lunar product for LEO requires mature ISRU **plus** mass driver delivery, or alternatively products with value density high enough that shipping cost is not the binding economic constraint.

q7's independent first-principles derivation of mass-driver capital lands at approximately **$1,242 billion under BAU** for a 1 Mt/yr operational system [q7.c6], dominated by the multi-kilometer track at $800M/m lunar-construction-multiplied EMALS pricing — 96% of the total capital. Under industrial-explosion compression (~10× capital, ~10× engineering pace) the figure drops to $127B / ~6 years; under TAI-grade compression (~100× / ~100×) to $13.3B / ~1.5 years. The TAI endpoint **converges with q2's $10B capital assumption** [q7.c8] and with the 1979 NASA SP-428 figure inflated to 2026 dollars (~$13B [q7.c12]). The 1979 study was already describing a compressed-economics envelope that 46 years of historical engineering did not realize. The convergence is therefore not a coincidence — three independent estimates all describe **the same architectural envelope under engineering compression that has not occurred in the historical record**.

The cost leg is therefore conditional on the regime being TAI-grade compression, not merely industrial-explosion. Industrial-explosion delivers $127B / 6 years for the mass driver — still an order of magnitude above q2.c5's $10B assumption, and the time horizon to operational scale falls outside the planning window for most national programs. The $50/kg headline lives or dies on TAI economics.

### Throughput necessity — assembled across q1, q6

q6 brackets cislunar / LEO total mass demand across the three regimes with three orders of magnitude separating them: stall ~20,000 t cumulative 2026-2040 (~1,400 t/yr), BAU ~2,150,000 t (~143 kt/yr) with 93% from space-based data centres, TAI-compression ~42,900,000 t (~2.87 Mt/yr) with 93% from SDC at 1,000 GW deployed [q6.c2]. The SDC mass intensity decomposition [q6.c1] — approximately 40 t/MW continuous compute (5-10 t compute hardware + 15-25 t solar panels + 5-10 t radiators + 5-10 t structure/station-keeping) — is cross-corroborated by two independent engineering analyses, peraspera 30-50 t/MW [peraspera-2025-orbital-compute] and luminix 42 t/MW [luminix-2026-sdc], within ±25%.

Under TAI-compression, SDC mass demand alone exceeds plausible Earth-launch throughput by approximately an order of magnitude [q6.c6]. A Starship fleet operating at q1's optimistic-late assumptions — roughly 200 active reusable vehicles × 10 flights/yr per vehicle × 200 t payload — tops out at ~400 kt/yr of LEO delivery capacity. TAI-C SDC demand at ~2.67 Mt/yr is approximately 7× that ceiling. The arithmetic is hard. Lunar-sourced bulk mass is therefore **architecturally necessary**, not merely cost-attractive, for the structural half of the SDC mass budget — radiators, solar arrays, station-keeping mass, sintered regolith shielding. q3 confirms the materials feasibility of every item in that list at TRL 4-6 by 2026 under any non-stall regime [q3.c9].

The compute hardware half of the SDC budget — GPUs, memory, networking, power conditioning — remains Earth-launch-bound regardless. The point is not that lunar manufacturing replaces Earth launch entirely; it covers the structural half of the bulk-mass requirement while terrestrial fabs continue to deliver the high-value-density compute hardware. q6 captures this as "necessary but not sufficient" [q6.c6] — the framing distinction matters for synthesis: lunar manufacturing under TAI-C is structurally integrated with terrestrial production, not a replacement of it.

The throughput leg is independent of the cost leg in a way worth emphasizing: even if mass-driver $/kg never reached terrestrial parity, the throughput ceiling under TAI-C still forces the architecture. A lunar mass driver at $300/kg (above q1's optimistic-late) would still be load-bearing on the TAI-C demand curve, because the alternative is "TAI-C SDC deployment cannot happen at the projected scale." The TAI-C regime is therefore over-determined for lunar manufacturing — both legs hold, and either leg alone would suffice.

## The regime split is sharper than smooth interpolation

The cross-consistency pass surfaces a load-bearing finding from the q1↔q6 coupling: **the BAU regime is not a smooth interpolation between stall and TAI-C; it is conditional on which side of the internal-cost-vs-list-price gap propagates to demand-driver decisions.**

q1's internal cost ($107-466/kg under partial-reuse) and q1's commercial list price ($7,000/kg rising at $500/kg/yr) differ by a factor of 10-15×. Which figure propagates depends on (a) whether SpaceX uses Starship for SpaceX-internal payloads — in which case the relevant cost is internal — or (b) whether commercial customers pay list prices, in which case demand-driver decisions key off the higher number. Under regime (a), BAU SDC and depot demand assumptions hold; under regime (b), BAU SDC compresses from ~50 GW to ~10-20 GW by 2040, total BAU LEO demand falls from ~143 kt/yr to ~50 kt/yr, and the "lunar manufacturing has a real BAU market" argument weakens substantially.

The TAI-C regime is unaffected by this distinction because it is gated by internal cost (SpaceX-internal use of Starship for its own AI infrastructure) and AI compute growth, **not** by commercial list pricing. The regime split is therefore not a continuous demand curve; it is a discontinuous step at the point where Starship internal economics versus Starship commercial economics diverge. Synthesis should treat BAU as two sub-regimes — **BAU-internal-cost** (high demand, lunar manufacturing has a market) and **BAU-list-price** (low demand, lunar manufacturing thesis weak) — rather than a single curve.

## What binds the answer more tightly than capital

Three engineering risks deserve separate surfacing because they bind the headline outside the framework of TAI compression:

**The cycle-life gap.** Mass-driver electromagnetic launchers at the 1 Mt/yr scale require approximately 10⁸ shots over a 20-year operating life; at Handmer's 10 Mt/yr nameplate, approximately 10⁹ shots [q7.c10]. State-of-art demonstrated EM-launcher cycle life is approximately 10² to 10³ shots. The Navy's Electromagnetic Railgun program was cancelled in 2021 after approximately $500 million of investment, with cycle life and barrel wear as the binding failure modes. The 5-7 orders of magnitude gap between demonstrated and required cycle life is **independent of capital cost and TAI compression** — TAI compresses labor, capital, and time, but not materials-science breakthroughs. Closing the gap requires advances in coil-winding insulation, projectile/rail interaction, and possibly entirely new launcher architectures (gas-coupled, multi-stage, sub-stage refurbishment cycles). This is a pre-condition the report must surface; without it, q7's TAI-grade $13.3B and q2's $50/kg headline are both contingent on a research outcome that has not been demonstrated and is not in any current funded program.

**The polar-ice gate.** q2's aggressive-ISRU chemical scenarios assume hydrolox propellant from lunar polar ice. q3.c4 specifies that polar ice is the only lunar-derivable rocket-fuel hydrogen route, gated geologically by polar resource characterization. PRIME-1 returned limited data from IM-2 in February 2025; VIPER's launch was rescheduled to late 2027 under Blue Origin's contract. If VIPER returns unfavorable results — polar ice present but uneconomic to extract at scale, or distributed in ways that make per-kg extraction cost prohibitive — q2's aggressive-ISRU column collapses to its Earth-imports-only column, and q2's mid-era figures rise from $1,303-8,912/kg to $1,303-13,029/kg. The hydrolox-based architecture also covers q5's lunar-base propellant assumption [q5 ↔ q3 dependency]. **VIPER late-2027 is the single largest near-term piece of empirical information that will move the report's headline.**

**The crewed-occupation 12-month floor.** q5.c7's M6 milestone — first crewed sustained occupation of the lunar surface base — requires a 12-month integration by definition. TAI compression accelerates the milestone clock between events but cannot compress a milestone whose definition IS the integration of 12 calendar months. The "TAI architecture in months not years" framing must therefore explicitly carve out crewed-occupation-gated milestones from the compression curve. The implication: even under TAI-grade compression, the buildup-to-net-positive-export endpoint requires no less than approximately 12-24 months from launch, set by M6's irreducible floor — not by capital or labor availability.

## The φ-threshold qualifier matters

q4's competitiveness framework hinges on the φ threshold (production mass ratio required for lunar absolute advantage at the destination). Metzger's mid-range value of approximately 35 [q4.c6] is the canonical anchor. q3's MRE reactor productivity [q3.c13] gives instantaneous full-plant φ of approximately 10-20 per year — **which does not clear 35**. Only **cumulative multi-year integration** (decade-scale operation summing reactor output) clears the threshold. Synthesis must preserve this qualifier; shortening to "MRE clears the φ threshold" overstates the case and reads as a year-1 economic argument rather than a steady-state-over-time argument.

The implication interacts with the financing structure of any lunar manufacturing program. Year-1 economics are dominated by capital amortization and ramp-up productivity; only after several years of mature operation does the φ-threshold framework deliver absolute advantage at the destination. A program structure that demands net-positive return in year 1 will reject lunar manufacturing under Metzger's framework regardless of regime; a program that amortizes over a 15-20 year horizon — closer to national-infrastructure timescales than venture-capital timescales — finds lunar manufacturing viable under TAI-C and arguably under industrial-explosion. The framework, in other words, is also a statement about the type of capital required.

## Confidence per finding

- **BAU/IE/TAI/stall regime decomposition as the proper organizing axis:** high. The framework is consistent across all seven leaves; calendar-year framings (e.g. "by 2040") consistently failed Codex audits across q1-q7 under anti-pattern #11.
- **Two-legged crossover (cost + throughput necessity) under TAI-grade:** medium-high. Each leg is independently sourced; both legs are conditional on TAI-grade compression actually occurring within the planning horizon, which is itself a load-bearing premise the report does not establish (it is the regime hypothesis, not a derived conclusion).
- **$50/kg mass-driver vs $59/kg Starship margin:** medium. Both numbers are individually low to medium-low confidence (q2.c5 depends on the $10B capital assumption; q1's optimistic case depends on aggressive refurbishment assumptions). The qualitative claim that "mass-driver+SEP closes within a factor of 2 of optimistic-late Starship under TAI-grade" is robust to ±50% movements in either headline.
- **Throughput necessity (TAI-C SDC > 10× Earth-launch ceiling):** medium-high. The 40 t/MW SDC mass intensity is cross-corroborated; the 400 kt/yr Earth-launch ceiling is bracketed by the Starship operational assumptions but could move ±2× either way under different cadence assumptions.
- **BAU full architecture $1.4-1.7T:** medium. q5 base ($150-400B) and q7 mass driver ($1,242B BAU) are individually anchored on sourced priors with substantial spread; the additive framing is supported by claim-scope, not contested.
- **Cycle-life gap as binding engineering risk:** medium-high. The 5-7 OOM gap is documented in q7's source review; the inference that TAI compression alone doesn't close it is reasoning, not measurement.
- **Polar-ice gate as binding near-term empirical variable:** high. VIPER's late-2027 schedule is well-anchored; the structural argument that q2 aggressive-ISRU depends on its outcome is direct.

## Limitations

The synthesis relies on the BAU/IE/TAI/stall regime decomposition as the organizing axis. If the actual trajectory is something else — for instance, an asymmetric compression where engineering pace accelerates faster than capital allocation, or vice versa — the answer reorganizes around different cross-leaf couplings. The framework is the best available organizing axis given current acceleration uncertainty; it is not a forecast.

The throughput-necessity leg depends on SDC demand projections at the TAI-C scale [q6.c2]. q6's Cote bandwidth-ceiling caveat [q6.c14] caps TAI-C SDC at ~200 GW versus the 1,000 GW headline if optical inter-satellite links and training-workload routing do not mitigate the orbital comms bottleneck. A factor-of-5 reduction in TAI-C SDC demand would still leave it above Earth-launch ceiling but only marginally, weakening the throughput-necessity leg to "throughput-attractive" rather than "throughput-necessary."

q7's regime-compression factors are speculative scenario priors, not externally validated. The Codex pass-02 audit appropriately downgraded the confidence on these terms. If TAI compression delivers only 10× rather than 100× capital reduction, q7's TAI mass-driver capital rises to $130B and q2's $50/kg headline collapses. The mass-driver leg is therefore particularly sensitive to the regime hypothesis it presupposes.

Several primary sources remain incompletely fetched: Citigroup 2040 forecast (q1), Coutts-Sowers paywalled (q2), Lewis NTRS binary-only (q2), AIAA 2025-4123 paywalled (q7), O'Neill-Kolm Acta 1980 PDF (q7). Headline numbers do not depend critically on these, but a future iteration with direct primary access would tighten confidence on several supporting claims.

The φ-threshold framework is model-contingent on Metzger's specific architectural assumptions [q4.c6]. Alternative TEAs (Charania-DePascuale, Jones, Pelech [q4.c8]) disagree on M_K mass estimates and economies-of-scale assumptions by factors of 2-4×. The synthesis treats Metzger's mid-range as canonical; this is a defensible choice given Metzger's seven-prior-TEA review [q4.c13] but not the only defensible one.

## What would change the answer

- **VIPER late-2027 returning unfavorable polar-ice results** — q2 aggressive-ISRU collapses; mass-driver projectile materials route via q3 basalt sintering survives but the lunar-base propellant economics weaken.
- **A cycle-life breakthrough in EM launchers** — closes the q7.c10 gap, makes BAU/IE mass-driver capital figures more defensible, lowers the regime conditioning of the cost leg.
- **TAI-grade compression failing to materialize within the planning horizon** — collapses both legs of the crossover. Synthesis answer becomes "no, under BAU, lunar manufacturing does not beat Earth launch for orbital infrastructure within 2026-2040."
- **Starship operational performance materially below q1's partial scenario** — raises q1's L_p, shifts q2's relative competitiveness (Earth-imports-only and aggressive-ISRU columns become more attractive in relative terms), but does not change the TAI-C throughput-necessity argument.
- **SDC demand materializing closer to McCalip's 3.2× cost-multiple skepticism than Handmer's 2× optimism** — flattens the BAU-TAI-C demand spread, weakens the throughput-necessity leg.

## Headline restated

**Under TAI-grade compression, lunar manufacturing beats Earth launch for orbital infrastructure on two independent grounds: cost (mass-driver $50/kg vs Starship $59/kg, with cycle-life gap as binding engineering risk) and throughput necessity (TAI-C SDC demand 10× Earth-launch ceiling, with lunar bulk mass architecturally necessary for ~50% of SDC mass budget). Under business-as-usual, neither leg holds: mass driver doesn't reach Mt-scale in time, and demand fits inside Earth-launch capacity. Under stall, the thesis collapses for lack of demand. The polar-ice gate (VIPER late-2027) and the cycle-life gap (5-7 OOM beyond demonstrated) are the two largest near-term variables that will move the answer. The framework is the deliverable; the headline number is conditional on the regime obtaining.**
